The City That Changed Overnight

San Francisco, a city bustling with tourists, restaurants are packed, and limitless activities to explore. But on an evening in March 2020, the city took a turn, a month we all remember: the dreadful start of the COVID-19 pandemic. Streets emptied, people boarded up in their homes, and just like that, the city was unrecognizable. Everything changed, from tourism levels dropping to the crime patterns changing.

Today we will explore how crime shifted as our beloved city changed forever. The San Francisco Police Department meticulously recorded incident reports ranging from 2018 to 2023, giving adequate data to analyze this change. The crimes to highlight include vandalism, prostitution, assault, disorderly conduct, burglary, stolen property, and fraud. So no, crime didn’t just stop or move online as we may have expected: it evolved from public spaces to private, opportunistic ones.

The Divergence Mystery

Now as expected, overall crime initially dropped as people figured out how to adapt to a new way of living; street crimes, such as prostitution and disorderly conduct, dropped off the charts. These crimes created a clear "V-shape" in the charts, which is unsurprising. Now while we might think that fraud would increase due to the shift to a more digital way of life with Zoom and online shopping becoming the new normal, it actually decreased significantly during COVID.

Figure 1: A City Transformed: How COVID-19 Reshaped SF Crime. These eight panels show the variety in change of different crime types in response to the implementation of “stay at home” policies. While some crimes such as disorderly conduct and assault show a clear v-shape as expected, others such as prostitution permanently drop. Burglary and fraud remained stubbornly high, due to the geographic shift in the city's life leading to more isolated and digital targets.

This dip in local fraud reports suggests that while the world moved to Zoom, the SFPD's data captures a more 'analog' side of the pandemic, focusing on crimes that required a physical presence rather than the invisible scams tracked by federal agencies like the FBI. However, burglary is the one crime that refused to drop. Zooming in on burglary, we find the most unexpected change: people were home ALL the time, so how come burglary levels were at an all-time high? A study in the Journal of Experimental Criminology investigated whether crime changed spatially due to lockdowns and found that while residential burglary decreased by 23.5%, non-residential incidents increased by 37.8%.

The "Guardian" Theory in Action

This can be linked to the Routine Activity Theory first proposed in the American Sociological Review, which posits that three elements must converge for a crime to occur: a motivated offender, a suitable target, and the absence of a capable guardian. During the mandatory lockdowns starting in March 2020, people were constantly home, acting as "guardians" for their residences.

Figure 2: Interactive Heat Map: 2020 Burglary Hotspots. Against what we initially expected, crime didn’t disappear, it just relocated. Commercial and workplace hubs such as SoMa and the Financial District became targets due to their vacancy. The opportunity was there, the eyes of workers and security were gone, thus crime levels in these areas grew. Zoom in to see how specific areas reacted.

When looking at the heatmap, notice the stark 'cooling' of residential districts like the Sunset and Richmond. These neighborhoods became protected as the city's workforce stayed home. In contrast, the 'heat' of crime is most prominent in commercial spots such as the Financial District, which for the first time in history, had no "eyes on the street" after 5:00 PM. Desks were empty and doors were unwatched, providing the perfect opportunity for non-residential burglary to thrive.
